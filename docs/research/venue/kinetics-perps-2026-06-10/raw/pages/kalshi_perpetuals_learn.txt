They use leverage, and they never expire.\n\nPerpetuals track an asset's price. Go long if you expect it to rise, short if you expect it to fall. Unlike a traditional futures contract that expires on a set date, Perpetuals have no expiry — you hold the position as long as your collateral can back it, and close it whenever you choose. No crypto ever changes hands.\n\nSince perpetual futures never expire and require less upfront capital than traditional futures, they can be a simpler and cheaper way to open a position. Leverage does increase the risk.\n\nHow Perpetuals compare to spot trading\n\nSpot trading means owning the real asset directly. Perpetuals trade the price instead, with leverage, the ability to go short, an 8-hour funding rate, and built-in liquidation all part of how the contract works.\n\n\tSPOT TRADING\tPERPETUALS\nOwn the asset\tYes\tNo\nProfit when price falls\tNo\tYes — by going short\nLeverage\tNo\tYes\nExpiration\tNone\tNone\nForced closure\tNo\tYes — via liquidation\nHow Perpetuals compare to prediction markets\n\nKalshi offers both. The closest equivalent to \"buying,\" with leverage applied and no asset held.\n\nShort — you profit if the price falls. Not possible in standard spot trading, where gains require prices to rise.\n\n03SIZING\n\nLeverage & margin\n\nLeverage determines how much exposure your collateral controls, which amplifies both potential gain and potential loss.\n\nTo open a position, you deposit collateral (also called margin). At 5× leverage, $1,000 of margin controls a $5,000 position — a 10% move in the asset becomes a 50% move in your collateral, in either direction.\n\nLEVERAGE ON $1,000 MARGIN\n5×\n1×\n2×\n3×\n4×\n5×\n6×\n7×\n8×\n9×\n10×\nYOUR MARGIN\n$1,000\nPOSITION SIZE\n$5,000\n\nA 10% move in the asset changes your equity by \n±$500\n(50% of your initial deposit)\n\nThe leverage examples are mathematical in nature and are not intended to imply that customers have achieved or may achieve similar results.\n\nIsolated margin\n\nIsolated margin assigns collateral to one position. If that position is liquidated, only the assigned collateral is at risk — the rest of your account is unaffected.\n\n04CONTROLS\n\nRisk controls\n\nTwo order types are most commonly used to keep a leveraged trade under control. Both are set when you open the position.\n\nTake profit\n\nA take profit closes your position automatically when the price reaches a specified gain target, locking in the gain so a subsequent reversal cannot give it back.\n\n$60,000\n$50,000\n$40,000\nTAKE PROFIT\nENTRY\nSTOP LOSS\n+$10,000\n\nBitcoin rises from $50,000 to $60,000. The position closes automatically at the take-profit level — gain locked at +$10,000.\n\nStop loss\n\nA stop loss closes your position automatically if the price moves against you past a specified level, capping the most you can lose on the trade. Widely used on leveraged positions because losses compound faster than on unleveraged ones.\n\n$60,000\n$50,000\n$40,000\nTAKE PROFIT\nENTRY\nSTOP LOSS\n−$10,000\n\nBitcoin falls from $50,000 to $40,000. Without them, a leveraged position can lose value faster than you can react in a volatile market.\n\n05THE MECHANISM\n\nThe funding rate\n\nBecause Perpetuals never expire, there is no settlement date to pull the contract price back toward the underlying asset. The broker closes the position automatically to prevent the account from going negative.\n\nThe higher the leverage, the smaller the adverse price move required to trigger liquidation. At 5× leverage, that's roughly a 20% adverse move; at 10×, roughly 10%. Liquidation is calculated against the mark price — an aggregated reference drawn from multiple spot markets — which reduces the risk of liquidation from brief price spikes on a single exchange.\n\nEXAMPLE SCENARIO\n\nAssume a $1,000 deposit at 5× leverage, opening a long with Bitcoin at $50,000.
